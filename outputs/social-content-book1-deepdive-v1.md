## SOURCE ANGLE NOTE

This pack is built from an external narrator's framing of Book 1 — a third-party "deep dive" summary voice, distinct from Baron's internal voice or Olivia's dialogue used elsewhere. That framing gives us permission to say the hard parts out loud: the exhaustion, the substance abuse, the closure, the recovery. This is the arc our prequel content and prior Book 1 posts haven't touched yet. Community and sobriety are the hook here — not the food, not the meet-cute.

**Spoiler policy:** The crisis and recovery are structural to the book's marketing (they're in the back-cover-adjacent summary), but the *specifics* of the breaking point and the *how* of the recovery are treated as spoiler territory in reader-facing posts. Tension is named; resolution mechanics are protected until the CTA.

### Facebook Post 1 — The Deep Dive Reframe (Full Story Arc)
**Content Type:** Behind-the-Story / Reader Connection

I want to talk about what Culinary Crescendo is actually about — not the elevator pitch, the real one.

Yes, it's a love story. Yes, it's about a sous chef and a blues singer who fall for each other while building a restaurant out of a shipping container. That part is true, and it's the part people expect.

But here's the part that surprises readers: the book doesn't stop at "they built something beautiful together." It keeps going into what building something beautiful actually costs.

Baron and Olivia get Bayouboujee open. It works. People come. The music plays. And then the weight of running two demanding lives at once — a kitchen and a stage, a business and a relationship — starts to grind them down. Exhaustion turns into something harder to name. Substance abuse creeps in. The partnership that built the dream starts to threaten it.

There's a financial and emotional breaking point. The doors close, at least for a while.

And then the part of the story I'm proudest of: their friends don't let it end there. Theron and Jamara step in and help rebuild what Baron and Olivia couldn't hold up alone. Not because it's owed to them. Because that's what community does.

By the end, Baron and Olivia don't just get Bayouboujee back. They get free — sober, whole, and finally able to trust their own creation to the people who love it, so they can go live a life they never had time for while they were building it.

That's the story. Triumph, collapse, community, freedom. In that order.

If you've only heard the love-story version of this book, I hope this gives you a reason to pick it up for the other half.

Culinary Crescendo: A Symphony of Love in the Big Easy. Available now from Lejohn Richburg III and FuturIQ.

### Facebook Post 2 — Community as the Real Twist
**Content Type:** Reader Connection / Theme Post

Every version of this story I could have written had a version where Baron and Olivia save themselves.

That's not the version I wrote.

The version I wrote has them hit a wall they can't push through alone — financially, emotionally, all of it at once — and it's their friends who carry them the rest of the way. Theron runs the kitchen he was never supposed to have to run. Jamara manages a business from across the country because Olivia needed her to.

Nobody in this book gets saved by grinding harder. They get saved by people who show up.

I think that's the truest thing in the whole book. Maybe the truest thing I know, period.

Culinary Crescendo: A Symphony of Love in the Big Easy — out now.

### Facebook Post 3 — Sobriety and Freedom (Author Voice)
**Content Type:** Behind-the-Story / Author Voice

I didn't want to write a book where two people build a dream and the story just ends at the ribbon-cutting.

Anybody can build something. The harder, more honest story is what happens when the thing you built almost becomes the thing that breaks you — and what it takes to come back from that clean.

Baron and Olivia's ending isn't "they kept the restaurant." It's that they got to choose, on their own sober terms, what freedom looked like next. They handed Bayouboujee to the people who'd fought to save it, and they went out into the world to live.

That's the ending I wanted for them. Not an empire. A life.

Culinary Crescendo: A Symphony of Love in the Big Easy. Lejohn Richburg III. FuturIQ.

If this is the first time you're hearing the full shape of this story, welcome — I think you'll want to read it now.
