### Facebook Post 1 — Behind-the-Story (Long Form)
**Content Type:** Behind-the-Story / Author Voice

There is a character in this book named Melpomene.

He is the Executive Chef at the Apothecary, the restaurant where Baron Vieux works as sous chef. He is demanding. He is proud. And he is quietly threatened by the competence of the man he is supposed to be leading.

After a particularly difficult service — one Baron saved almost entirely on his own — Melpomene pulls him aside and says:

"The service was different and not consistent with how we run things here. These are my creations. They cannot be altered. If you want to run a faulty kitchen, then you should start your own."

He meant it as a rebuke.

What he could not have known is that Baron had already filed the LLC. Already registered the domain. Already sketched the blueprint for a converted shipping container on a gravel lot off Magazine Street.

Melpomene thought he was ending a conversation.

He was starting one.

This is the moment the Scarlet Foundation is built. Not from money. Not from connections. From an insult delivered by the wrong man to someone who had already done the work.

In the mythology of this book, every character is named after a New Orleans street. Melpomene — the Greek muse of tragedy. Named correctly, as it turns out.

### Facebook Post 2 — Reader Connection / Character Depth
**Content Type:** Storytelling — The Walk Home / Calliope

Every night after service, Baron walks past a bar.

There's a bartender there. Sharp eyes. Steady hands. She watches people the way a sculptor watches stone — looking for what's already there.

Her name, in this story, is Calliope. Named after the Greek muse of epic poetry. The one who believed things were worth telling before anyone else agreed.

She has watched Baron fix broken POS systems mid-service from across the room. Watched him build a website at 1 AM on his phone while still in his apron. Watched him carry the weight of someone else's kitchen without complaint.

And on the nights when the weight gets heavy enough that it shows on his face — only a little, but she sees it — she says one thing:

"You're closer than you think, Baron."

Between them, there is a private language. When Baron has had a night that would have broken most people, when the city has leaned on him and the kitchen has taken everything he had, he stops at her bar and says two words:

"I hate it here."

Which means: thank you. Which means: I am still standing. Which means: I see the dream even from here.

Every story about building something real has a person like Calliope in it. The one who believes in the vision when you are too tired to believe in it yourself.

She earned her chapter.

### Facebook Post 3 — Origin Story / Community Post
**Content Type:** Storytelling — Full Arc Overview

Before I tell you about this book, let me tell you about the resume of the man at the center of it.

Baron Vieux started at a coffee shop on Decatur Street as a teenager. Not a culinary school. A coffee shop — where he learned to read people before they reached the counter, and stayed forty-five minutes after close to resolve a $3.40 drawer discrepancy because the numbers had to be right.

He went into telecom. Became the top sales rep in South Texas. Learned de-escalation not from a textbook but from standing between an angry customer and a situation no one else wanted to handle.

He ran a cafe as General Manager. Cut inventory costs by forty percent. Built the website. Managed the delivery platforms. Became a ServSafe Certified Instructor.

Then COVID hit. The professional world went remote. Baron Vieux walked into the ACE Hotel New Orleans and applied for a line cook job. From scratch. Starting over.

He cooked his way to the Four Seasons. Triple-A Five Diamond. Zero margin for error. That is where the Scarlet Redfish technique was born.

And then — the Apothecary. Sous Chef under a man named Melpomene. Where everything accumulated.

Where the insult finally came.

This is a book about what it takes to build something on your own terms. Not the glamorous version. The real version — permit offices, oven failures, missed soccer games, a reduction that took four months to perfect, a vision sketched in a notebook at 1 AM.

Culinary Crescendo: The Scarlet Foundation / Satire of New Orleans tells this story from the beginning.

Baron Vieux is fictional. His resume is not.
