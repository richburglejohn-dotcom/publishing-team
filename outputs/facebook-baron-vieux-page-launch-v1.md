### Post 1 — Page Launch / Introduction

I don't usually explain myself. People either see it or they don't.

But since this is a first post, I'll make an exception.

My name is Baron Vieux. I'm a sous chef in the French Quarter. I've spent longer in kitchens than most people spend at any one job, and longer than that learning that the kitchen was never really the point — it was just where I finally got to build something instead of just running someone else's numbers.

I don't have a restaurant yet. I have a filed LLC, a registered domain, and a blueprint for a gravel lot off Magazine Street that nobody's given me permission to build on.

I wasn't asking for permission.

This page is where I'll talk about the food, the city, and the work. Follow along.

### Post 2 — The Elixir

There's a reduction I keep in my head and nowhere else.

Roasted tomato. Smoked guajillo, charred over the coil until it stops smelling like a pepper and starts smelling like a decision. Worcestershire. Peychaud's. Apple cider vinegar. A dark roux built from scratch, because the shortcut version tastes like a shortcut. Cane sugar. White pepper. Cayenne. Reduced. Then reduced again.

It took four months of days off to get it right. I never wrote it down.

Some people keep their signature recipe in a notebook so someone can inherit it someday.

I keep mine because it's mine.

### Post 3 — On Ownership

Somebody told me once that if I didn't like how a kitchen was run, I should start my own.

He meant it as an insult.

I've been turning it over ever since, and every time I do, it sounds less like an insult and more like the only good advice anyone's given me in years.

The domain's live. The LLC's filed. I'm still cooking on someone else's line for now.

For now.

---

### Post 4 — The Storage Room

After service, there's a room in the back where the crew keeps a bottle nobody talks about and everybody knows is there.

That's not a metaphor. That's just what keeps a kitchen crew a crew — the ten minutes after close where nobody's the boss and everybody's just tired in the same direction.

I've learned more in that room than in most of the professional training I paid for.

---

### Post 5 — Origin, Briefly

Before any of this, I was behind an espresso machine on Decatur Street, learning to read a person before they got to the counter.

Before that, telecom. Before that, nothing that looked like a kitchen at all.

I bring all of it to work with me every day — the systems thinking, the de-escalation, the part of me that still notices when a walk-in cooler is running two degrees warm before anyone else does.

None of it was wasted. All of it was preparation.
