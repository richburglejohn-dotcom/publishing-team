# The Scarlet Redfish — Plating & Presentation Reference
### Captured: 2026-07-08, from a Restaurant Week plating photo, the author's original (unpitched) menu proposal, and author confirmation
### Status: Confirmed — two versions on record, improvised version is canon

This was never fully detailed in Book 1 — the author was still developing it live during Restaurant Week as the book released, and never actually pitched the formal version to the head chef. This doc reconciles both versions so future writing (prequel, later books, marketing) stays consistent.

---

## Version A — As Actually Served (canonical)

This is what was plated and photographed during Restaurant Week, and is the version that should be treated as canon for the series.

- **Fish:** Skin-on redfish fillet. Skin seared in oil reserved from roasting garlic and onions for another one of the head chef's recipes — repurposed on the fly — to a deep, cracked, audible-crunch crisp. Finished with cracked black pepper.
- **Base:** A bed of tri-color marble potatoes — small roasted baby potatoes in gold, red-skinned, and deep purple/black — added to flesh out the plate.
- **Garnish:** Micro pea tendrils (substituted in when the produce order came up short).
- **The "Scarlet" accent:** Thin rings of red and orange Fresno chilies, scattered across the plate and tucked beneath the fish. This is where the dish's name comes from visually, not just from the glaze.
- **The glaze:** Honey, white wine, rosemary sprigs, and lemon zest, built in a squeeze bottle. **This is not the book's canonical Scarlet Elixir** (the dark tomato-guajillo reduction from Ch. 7) — it's a separate, brighter preparation specific to this dish. Keep the two distinct in any future reference.

**The Signature Moment — The Sizzle Tray:** The glaze is meant to be splashed onto a hot sizzle tray tableside — it hits the hot metal in front of the guest, producing sizzle, steam, and aroma. This is the dish's "apothecary" theater: it reads like a tincture being administered, not a sauce being poured.

**Kitchen reality:** That service was short-staffed, and the tray couldn't get to the table hot in time. The glaze was splashed on at the pass instead and the dish sent out already plated — it still worked, but without the tableside moment. A true kitchen compromise, not the intended presentation, and good texture for a scene about the strain of running a kitchen shorthanded — very on-brand for Baron, who improvises under pressure rather than sending out something wrong.

## Version B — The Original Proposal (never pitched, not canon)

Found in `Scarlet_Elixir_Redfish_Proposal.pptx` — a full menu proposal deck the author built for "Apothecary Restaurant" but never actually presented to the head chef. Kept here for reference / possible future use (e.g. a "deleted scene" or bonus content), but should not be treated as canon unless the author says otherwise.

- **Vegetables:** Cippolini onions (caramelized for balsamic-like sweetness) and red Fresno peppers — no potatoes.
- **Garnish:** Shaved fennel and micro-parsley.
- **Method:** Convection oven, 425°F, high fan, fish skin-up on a parchment tray with pre-blanched vegetables. ~10-minute prep-to-plate: roast (2 min) → glaze applied to vegetables, back in for a 2-minute reduction (at ~8 min) → garnish (10 min).
- **The glaze:** Described as a "honeyed wine reduction."
- **Framing:** Pitched as "restorative cuisine" — anti-inflammatory fennel, vitamin-rich peppers, clean local protein — leaning hard into the Apothecary's healing/mystical branding. Gluten-free, hood-free (convection only, no gas), scalable one-pan prep for peak rushes.
- **Business case:** Proposed for the next seasonal rotation at an estimated $32–36 plate price.

**Why it didn't happen:** the produce order came up short on fennel and micro-parsley the day it needed to go out, and the author was short-staffed — so Version A is what actually reached the plate.
