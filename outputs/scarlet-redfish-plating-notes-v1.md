# The Scarlet Redfish — Plating & Presentation Reference
### Captured: 2026-07-08, from a Restaurant Week plating photo and author confirmation
### Status: Presentation confirmed. Full production recipe (temps, seasoning ratios, cook method) still needs author input.

This was never fully detailed in Book 1 — the author was still perfecting it live during Restaurant Week as the book released. This doc captures what's now confirmed so it can be used consistently in the prequel and any future editions.

---

## The Plate

- **Fish:** Skin-on redfish fillet, seared skin-side to a deep, cracked, audible-crunch crisp. Finished with cracked black pepper.
- **Base:** A bed of tri-color marble potatoes — small roasted baby potatoes in three colors: gold, red-skinned, and deep purple/black.
- **Garnish:** Pea shoots and delicate herb sprigs laid over the fillet.
- **The "Scarlet" accent:** Thin rings of red and orange Fresno chilies, scattered across the plate and tucked beneath the fish. This is where the dish's name comes from visually, not just from the Elixir.

## The Signature Moment — The Sizzle Tray

The Scarlet Elixir is not ladled under the fish back in the kitchen. It's meant to be splashed onto a **hot sizzle tray tableside** — the reduction hits the hot metal in front of the guest, producing sizzle, steam, and a burst of aroma. This is the dish's "apothecary" theater: it reads like a tincture being administered, not a sauce being poured. It's the moment that ties the dish back to the Apothecary's whole restorative/alchemical framing.

**Kitchen reality:** this move needs enough front-of-house staff to get a hot tray to the table before it cools. During a short-staffed Restaurant Week service, Baron splashed the Elixir onto the fish directly at the pass instead and sent it out already plated — the dish still worked, but without the tableside moment. That's a true kitchen compromise, not the intended presentation, and could make good material for a scene about the strain of running Bayouboujee shorthanded.
